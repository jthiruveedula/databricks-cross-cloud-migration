# Risk assessment

[Asset inventory](/discovery/asset-inventory) and [dependency mapping](/discovery/dependency-mapping) tell you what exists and how it connects. Risk assessment turns that into a score you can actually sequence waves by — without it, [wave planning](/execution/wave-planning) is just intuition with extra steps.

## Risk dimensions

| Dimension | Low risk | High risk |
|---|---|---|
| Business criticality | Internal reporting, non-customer-facing | Revenue-impacting, customer-facing, regulatory-reportable |
| Data sensitivity | Public or internal data | PII, PHI, PCI-scoped data |
| Dependency count | Standalone table/job, no downstream consumers | Hub table with many downstream jobs/dashboards |
| Data volume/velocity | Small batch, daily | Large volume or continuous streaming |
| Custom tooling | Standard Workflows/DLT | Bespoke orchestration, non-standard cluster configs, legacy Hive |
| Owner engagement | Actively maintained, responsive team | Orphaned, no clear owner, tribal knowledge only |
| Rollback complexity | Stateless, easily re-run | Stateful streaming, hard to replay exactly |

<Callout client:visible variant="warning" title="Orphaned workloads are the highest-risk category, not the lowest">
  It's tempting to deprioritize a workload with no responsive owner as "low activity, low risk." The opposite is usually true — no owner means no one to validate the migration worked, no one to explain undocumented behavior, and no one to notice quietly broken output until a downstream consumer complains weeks later.
</Callout>

## Scoring model

<CodeBlock client:visible language="python" filename="risk_score.py" code={`WEIGHTS = {
    "criticality": 3,
    "sensitivity": 3,
    "dependency_count": 2,
    "volume_velocity": 1,
    "custom_tooling": 2,
    "owner_engagement": 2,  # inverted: unengaged owner = high score = high risk
}

def risk_score(workload: dict) -> int:
    return sum(WEIGHTS[dim] * workload["ratings"][dim] for dim in WEIGHTS)

# Ratings are 1 (low) - 3 (high) per dimension, gathered during discovery interviews
# and from the dependency graph -- not guessed from the asset name alone.
`} />

Bucket the resulting scores into three tiers to drive wave assignment:

- **Low risk** — pilot and early waves. Builds confidence and validates tooling.
- **Medium risk** — mid-sequence waves, once tooling and process are proven.
- **High risk** — late waves, with extra validation time and a named rollback owner, or a dedicated blue-green approach instead of a standard wave cutover.

## Risk register

Track every high- and medium-risk item as a standing register entry, not just a scoring artifact you compute once — see the [risk register template](/templates/risk-register) for the tracked format (owner, mitigation, status, review date).

## Validation

- Have the workload's actual owning team review its risk rating — a rating assigned purely from discovery tooling output misses context (e.g. "this looks standalone but it's the input to a quarterly regulatory report").
- Cross-check high-data-sensitivity workloads against the [audit and compliance](/security/audit-and-compliance) requirements before wave assignment, not after.
- Re-score after the pilot wave — real migration friction discovered in the pilot should adjust risk ratings for similar workloads still pending.

## Rollback

Risk assessment produces no environment changes; there's nothing to roll back. A wrong risk rating is a planning-quality issue — fix it by re-scoring and re-sequencing, not by reverting infrastructure.

## Automation opportunity

- Compute dependency-count and volume/velocity ratings directly from the [dependency mapping](/discovery/dependency-mapping) graph and system tables instead of manual estimation.
- Auto-populate the [risk register](/templates/risk-register) from the scoring output so medium/high-risk items don't rely on someone remembering to transcribe them.
