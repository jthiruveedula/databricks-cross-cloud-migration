# Business validation

[Technical validation](/validation/technical-validation) and [security validation](/validation/security-validation) confirm the platform is correct and secure. Business validation confirms it's actually usable by the people who depend on it — a dashboard that's technically correct but confusingly different from what business users expect is still a migration failure from their perspective.

<Callout client:visible variant="prerequisite" title="Business validation needs real business users, not just engineering sign-off">
  An engineer confirming a query returns the right numbers is not the same as a business stakeholder confirming a report matches what they'll present to their leadership. Get the actual consumer to validate, not a proxy.
</Callout>

## What business validation covers

| Area | Who validates | What "done" looks like |
|---|---|---|
| Dashboards and reports | Business/analytics consumers | Visual output matches source for the same period, reviewed by the actual audience |
| Scheduled reports/exports | Report recipients | Recipients confirm they received the expected report on the expected schedule |
| Ad hoc query access | Analysts/data consumers | Analysts can run their typical queries with expected performance |
| ML model outputs | Model owners/business stakeholders | Predictions reviewed for reasonableness against known cases, not just statistical parity |
| SLA and performance | Application/business owners | Reports/dashboards load within the time users expect, not just within a technical benchmark |
| Regulatory/compliance reporting | Compliance stakeholders | Reports required for external filing are verified accurate and on schedule |

## Structuring the validation session

1. Identify the actual business consumer for each critical workload — not their manager, not the engineering liaison.
2. Have them exercise the target system doing their normal task (running their usual report, checking their usual dashboard), not a scripted demo.
3. Compare output against a known-good source result for the same period/inputs.
4. Capture explicit sign-off per workload, not a single blanket "business approved migration" statement — see [RACI](/templates/raci) for who's accountable for each sign-off.

<Callout client:visible variant="warning" title="Don't accept silence as sign-off">
  If a business stakeholder doesn't respond to a validation request, that is an open risk, not implicit approval. Escalate through the [RACI](/templates/raci) accountable owner rather than proceeding to cutover on an assumption.
</Callout>

## Validation

- Confirm sign-off was given by the actual consuming stakeholder for each critical workload, with evidence retained (email, signed document, or ticket) — see [audit and compliance](/security/audit-and-compliance) for retention expectations.
- Cross-check that every workload flagged high-criticality in [risk assessment](/discovery/risk-assessment) has a corresponding business sign-off — a missing sign-off on a high-criticality workload should block cutover.
- Verify SLA/performance expectations were validated against actual user perception (e.g. "loads fast enough"), not only a technical benchmark number.

## Rollback

If business validation surfaces a blocking issue, treat it the same as a failed technical or security validation — do not proceed to [cutover](/execution/cutover) for that workload. This blocks the go/no-go decision; it doesn't require an environment rollback since cutover for that workload hasn't happened yet.

## Automation opportunity

- Track sign-off status per workload in the same dashboard used for [risk register](/templates/risk-register) and wave tracking, so a missing business sign-off is visible alongside technical and security gates rather than in a separate email thread.
